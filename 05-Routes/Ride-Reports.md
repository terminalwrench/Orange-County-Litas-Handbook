# Ride Reports

Every completed ride should have a ride report. The report turns day-of experience into reusable chapter knowledge.

A route may look strong during planning but reveal issues once a real group rides it. Timing may be different than expected, parking may be harder with more bikes, a fuel stop may be too crowded, or a road section may feel less beginner-friendly than planned. Ride reports capture those lessons before they fade.

Use [Templates/Ride-Report-Template.md](Templates/Ride-Report-Template.md) after rides that may be repeated, adapted, or referenced by future leadership.

## Why Reports Matter

Ride reports help the chapter:

- Improve route descriptions.
- Adjust difficulty ratings.
- Identify recurring hazards.
- Preserve useful fuel, restroom, parking, and food information.
- Understand how timing changes with group size.
- Record rider feedback.
- Avoid repeating preventable planning issues.
- Give future ride leaders a stronger starting point.

## When to Create a Report

Create a report after:

- A new route is tested or completed.
- A recurring ride uses a revised route.
- A ride has unexpected traffic, weather, parking, or pacing issues.
- Rider feedback reveals confusion, discomfort, or a better option.
- The chapter discovers a helpful stop, hazard, or regroup location.
- Leadership wants to repeat the ride in a future season.

Reports do not need to be long. Clear, specific notes are more useful than a polished recap.

## What to Include

A useful ride report should document:

- Event name and date.
- Rider count.
- Weather and traffic conditions.
- What worked well.
- Any challenges or safety concerns.
- Rider feedback.
- Recommended route changes.
- Lessons learned for next time.

If the report affects a route file, update the route file and note the revision history.

## Storage

Store ride reports in a consistent location chosen by chapter leadership. If a report belongs to a specific route, link it from that route file. If a report affects multiple route categories, cross-reference it from the relevant planning notes.

Do not post private rider information, incident details, medical information, or sensitive member feedback in route reports. Keep reports practical, respectful, and useful for planning.
