# Route Planning Standards

Route planning turns a possible ride into a chapter-ready ride. The goal is not to make every route complicated. The goal is to know the route well enough to describe it honestly, lead it calmly, and adjust when conditions change.

Use these standards before adding a route to the library or approving it for a chapter event.

## Route Testing

Whenever possible, a route should be pre-ridden before it is used for a chapter event.

During testing, confirm:

- Road surface and lane condition.
- Construction, closures, detours, and traffic changes.
- Gravel, sand, water runoff, potholes, debris, or rough pavement.
- Freeway transitions, lane changes, and confusing intersections.
- Technical sections such as tight turns, steep grades, or blind corners.
- Safe pullouts and regroup areas.
- Actual travel time at a chapter-appropriate pace.
- Fuel, restroom, food, and parking availability.
- Cellular coverage concerns or areas where riders may lose connection.

If a route cannot be tested in person, leadership should validate it with a rider who knows the roads and review current conditions close to the event date. Untested routes should be described cautiously and should not be labeled beginner-friendly without direct confirmation.

## Regroup Locations

Regroup locations keep the ride organized without encouraging riders to chase the group.

A good regroup location:

- Is easy to identify from the road.
- Has legal and safe pull-off space.
- Does not block traffic, driveways, businesses, bike lanes, or emergency access.
- Allows the sweep to arrive before the group leaves.
- Gives the lead enough space to restart smoothly.
- Has room for the expected rider count.

Document regroup locations by name, cross streets, nearby landmarks, and any entry or exit notes. If a regroup point only works for small groups, state that clearly.

## Fuel Planning

Fuel planning should happen before the event is published.

Confirm:

- Whether riders must arrive with a full tank.
- Nearest fuel to the meetup point.
- Planned fuel stops for longer routes.
- Whether the fuel stop can handle multiple bikes without blocking pumps or traffic.
- Safe entry and exit.
- Restrooms, water, shade, and nearby waiting space.
- Backup fuel options if the planned stop is closed or crowded.

For most local rides, public details should include "arrive fueled and ready to ride." Longer rides should include planned fuel stops in the ride brief.

## Restroom Availability

Restroom access affects rider comfort, timing, and inclusivity.

Document:

- Restrooms at the meetup point, fuel stops, food stops, destination, and regroup areas.
- Whether restrooms are public, customer-only, seasonal, or limited.
- Any known long gaps without restroom access.
- Alternate stops if the primary location is closed or unavailable.

Do not assume a restroom exists because a business or park appears on a map.

## Parking Capacity

Parking should be reviewed before a route is approved.

Confirm:

- Motorcycle-friendly parking at meetup, stops, and destination.
- Approximate number of bikes the location can accommodate.
- Whether parking is free, paid, metered, permit-only, or time-limited.
- Entry and exit difficulty.
- Surface type, slope, tight turns, and low-speed handling concerns.
- Overflow or backup parking.

If parking is uncertain, include a destination-only option or choose a different destination.

## Beginner Considerations

Beginner-friendly means the whole ride experience supports newer riders, not just that the route is short.

Before marking a route beginner-friendly, confirm:

- Predictable roads and turns.
- Manageable distance and ride time.
- Minimal freeway complexity.
- Conservative pacing.
- Clear regroup points.
- Low-pressure destination and parking.
- A patient, experienced sweep.
- A plan for riders who want to meet at the destination instead.
- No technical sections that would surprise a newer rider.

If any section requires more confidence, label the ride honestly and explain the specific challenge.

## Communication

Route communication should be clear before the ride and repeated at the meetup.

Public event details should include:

- Route category and difficulty.
- Meetup time and kickstands-up time.
- Approximate distance and ride time.
- Freeway, canyon, mountain, night, or long-distance exposure.
- Fuel expectations.
- Destination and parking notes.
- Weather decision timing when relevant.

The pre-ride briefing should include:

- Ride Lead and Sweep names.
- Route overview.
- Regroup strategy.
- Planned stops.
- Formation expectations.
- What to do if separated.
- Reminder that riders should not exceed their comfort level to keep up.

Do not rely on a chat thread for safety-critical information once bikes are moving.

## Lead Responsibilities

The Ride Lead owns the route, pacing, and day-of decisions.

The lead should:

- Know the route before the ride begins.
- Arrive early.
- Check weather, traffic, and road conditions.
- Brief the group.
- Set a pace appropriate to the published difficulty.
- Pull over only where it is legal and safe.
- Wait for the sweep at regroup points.
- Adjust, shorten, reroute, or cancel if conditions change.
- Coordinate with the event host, partner, or destination contact when applicable.

A chapter ride should not include surprise route changes, hidden difficulty, or aggressive pacing.

## Sweep Responsibilities

The Sweep Rider is the final rider in the group and part of the safety plan.

The sweep should:

- Know the route and destination.
- Have the lead's phone number.
- Watch for riders who are delayed, separated, uncomfortable, or having mechanical issues.
- Stay behind the final rider unless safety requires otherwise.
- Communicate issues at regroup points.
- Help a rider exit the group safely if needed.
- Confirm the group is complete at planned stops.

Choose a sweep who is patient, experienced, and comfortable riding at the pace of the least confident rider.

## Contingency Planning

Every route should have a practical backup plan.

Plan for:

- Weather changes.
- Construction, closures, or unexpected traffic.
- A rider who wants to leave the ride.
- Mechanical problems.
- A fuel stop, restroom, restaurant, or destination that is closed.
- A group that is larger or smaller than expected.
- A route that is running late.
- An incident or emergency.

Contingency planning does not need to be dramatic. It should give leadership enough options to stay calm and make safe decisions.
