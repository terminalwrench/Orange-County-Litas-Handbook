# Ride Planning

Ride planning exists to make group motorcycle events clear, safe, and welcoming. A good ride plan protects the rider experience before the ride starts: route difficulty is honest, timing is realistic, and the group knows what to expect.

Use this with [Beginner-Rides.md](Beginner-Rides.md), [Weather-Contingencies.md](Weather-Contingencies.md), and [Templates/Ride-Template.md](Templates/Ride-Template.md).

## Destination Selection

Choose a destination that fits the riders, season, and purpose of the event.

Evaluate:

- Distance from the meetup point
- Parking availability for motorcycles
- Food, water, restrooms, and shade
- Road difficulty on the way in and out
- Traffic patterns by day and time
- Whether newer riders can meet at the destination instead
- Photo opportunities that do not create unsafe staging

For destination events such as large motorcycle shows, beach days, or partner gatherings, plan arrival timing around expected crowds and parking pressure.

## Route Testing

The route should be tested or validated before it is announced as a chapter ride.

Check:

- Road surface
- Construction
- Gravel, sand, water runoff, potholes, or debris
- Freeway transitions
- Tight turns or steep grades
- Cellular dead zones
- Safe pullouts
- Real travel time
- Gas and restroom availability

If the route cannot be tested in person, review it with a local rider who knows the roads and check current road conditions close to the event date.

## Beginner Considerations

Do not label a ride beginner friendly unless the full experience supports newer riders.

Beginner-friendly rides should have:

- Predictable roads
- Manageable distance
- Minimal freeway complexity
- Clear regroup points
- Conservative pace
- No pressure to keep up beyond legal and comfortable riding
- A patient sweep
- A destination option for riders who do not want to ride with the group

See [Beginner-Rides.md](Beginner-Rides.md) for the full beginner ride standard.

## Gas Stops

Plan gas before it becomes urgent.

Confirm:

- Nearest gas to the meetup point
- Whether riders should arrive with a full tank
- Gas stop timing for longer rides
- Whether the gas station can handle several bikes at once
- Safe entry and exit
- Restroom availability

For most local rides, the public details should say, "Arrive fueled and ready to ride." For longer rides, include the planned gas stop in the ride brief.

## Regroup Locations

Regroup points keep a ride organized without encouraging riders to chase the group.

Good regroup locations:

- Are easy to identify
- Have legal and safe pull-off space
- Do not block traffic, driveways, or emergency access
- Allow the sweep to arrive before the group leaves
- Give the lead enough room to restart smoothly

Announce regroup strategy during the pre-ride briefing. Riders should know that the group will wait at planned points.

## Communication

Ride communication should be simple and repeated in the right places.

Before the ride:

- Publish meetup time and kickstands-up time separately
- State difficulty, route style, and expected pace
- Name freeway, canyon, night, long-distance, or dirt/gravel exposure
- Share weather decision timing
- Remind riders to arrive fueled

At the meetup:

- Introduce Ride Lead and Sweep
- Review route, stops, and destination
- Explain staggered formation or single-file sections
- Confirm no one is pressured to ride beyond comfort
- Explain what to do if separated
- Confirm emergency contact process if one is being used

If using rider chat, keep it practical. Avoid relying on a chat thread for safety-critical instructions once bikes are moving.

## Lead Responsibilities

The Ride Lead owns the route and the pace.

Responsibilities:

- Validate the route
- Arrive early
- Check weather and road conditions
- Brief the group
- Set a pace appropriate to the event description
- Pull over only where it is legal and safe
- Wait for the sweep at regroup points
- Adjust or cancel the route if conditions change
- Coordinate with the day-of host or event owner

The lead should ride predictably. A chapter ride is not the place to surprise the group with sudden route changes, unannounced difficulty, or aggressive pacing.

## Sweep Responsibilities

The Sweep Rider is the final rider in the group and an active part of the safety structure.

Responsibilities:

- Know the route and destination
- Carry the lead's phone number
- Watch for riders who are struggling, delayed, or separated
- Communicate issues at regroup points
- Stay behind the final rider unless a safety issue requires action
- Help a rider exit the group safely if needed
- Confirm the group is complete at stops

The sweep should be patient, experienced, and comfortable riding at the pace of the least confident rider.

## Weather Review

Review weather at three points:

- When planning the event
- 48 to 24 hours before the event
- The morning of the event

Consider rain, excessive heat, wind, smoke, poor visibility, and road condition after weather. Use [Weather-Contingencies.md](Weather-Contingencies.md) for modification and cancellation guidance.

## Emergency Planning

The goal is not to make every ride heavy. The goal is to know what to do if something happens.

Prepare:

- Lead and sweep phone numbers
- Destination address
- Nearest major cross streets for regroup points
- Plan for mechanical issues
- Plan for a rider who wants to leave the ride
- Procedure for an incident or crash
- Local emergency number expectations

If an incident occurs, prioritize scene safety, emergency services, and calm communication. Do not post details publicly before the people involved have been cared for and leadership has accurate information.

## Arrival Timing

Build timing from the destination backward.

Account for:

- Rider check-in and briefing
- Late arrivals
- Gas stops
- Traffic
- Regroup waits
- Parking at destination
- Venue opening time or reservation time
- Partner event schedule

For public details, list:

- Meetup time
- Kickstands-up time
- Destination arrival estimate
- End time if known

When timing is tight, simplify the route rather than asking riders to rush.
