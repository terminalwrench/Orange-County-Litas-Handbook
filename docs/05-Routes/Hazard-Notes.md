# Hazard Notes

Hazard notes preserve route conditions that leaders should review before repeating a ride. The purpose is not to scare riders or over-document every imperfection. The purpose is to identify recurring issues that affect route choice, pacing, rider level, or day-of communication.

Hazards should be specific, current, and connected to a route, road section, stop, or season when possible.

## Common Hazards

Record recurring hazards such as:

- Gravel, sand, or debris.
- Rough pavement, potholes, or uneven lanes.
- Blind corners.
- Heavy traffic.
- Construction or detours.
- Limited parking.
- Water runoff.
- Steep grades.
- Tight turnarounds.
- Confusing freeway transitions.
- Areas with limited cellular service.

## Hazard Record

Use this structure when documenting a hazard:

```markdown
## Hazard Name or Road Section

- Route or category:
- Location:
- Hazard type:
- Direction of travel:
- Severity:
- Rider level concern:
- When it appears:
- How to brief riders:
- Recommended adjustment:
- Last confirmed:
- Notes:
```

## Severity

Use plain language when describing severity:

- Low: Worth briefing, but manageable with normal attention.
- Moderate: Affects pacing, lane position, regroup planning, or beginner suitability.
- High: May require rerouting, postponing, limiting rider level, or removing the route from use until reviewed.

Do not minimize hazards to make a route sound easier. Difficulty should be described honestly.

## Maintenance

Update hazard notes when:

- Construction begins or ends.
- Road surface changes.
- Parking access changes.
- A ride report identifies a new issue.
- A hazard is no longer present.
- Seasonal conditions change the risk.

If a hazard affects route approval, update the route file and planning checklist, not only this document.
