# Poker Runs

A poker run is a structured ride or drive event where participants visit checkpoints, collect cards or scores, and gather at the end for prizes and community time. It needs more operational control than a casual ride because timing, volunteers, scoring, and safety all depend on each other.

Use this with [Ride-Planning.md](Ride-Planning.md), [Special-Events.md](Special-Events.md), [Weather-Contingencies.md](Weather-Contingencies.md), and [Templates/Event-Brief.md](Templates/Event-Brief.md).

## Core Design

Decide early:

- Start location
- Number of checkpoints
- End location
- Ride format
- Participant cost, if any
- Prize categories
- Volunteer needs
- Whether cars, passengers, or non-riders can participate
- Charity or fundraising component

Keep the rules simple enough that volunteers can explain them consistently.

## Checkpoints

Checkpoints should be easy to find, legal to access, and safe for groups to enter and exit.

For each checkpoint, confirm:

- Address
- Parking
- Volunteer owner
- Setup time
- Close time
- Card or score process
- Restroom or water availability
- Traffic concerns
- Backup contact

Avoid checkpoints that require illegal parking, risky U-turns, or crossing heavy traffic on foot.

## Scoring

Choose one scoring method and document it before the event.

Options:

- Traditional poker hand
- Best hand and worst hand
- Point-based card draw
- Themed challenge points
- Bonus points for sponsor stops or raffle entries

Scoring needs:

- Participant scorecard
- Master score sheet
- Tie-breaker rule
- Final review owner
- Prize announcement timing

Do not change scoring rules mid-event unless safety or fairness requires it.

## Prizes

Prizes should be clear, appropriate, and ready before the event starts.

Confirm:

- Prize categories
- Sponsor or donor credit
- Where prizes are stored during the event
- Who announces winners
- What happens if a winner is not present

For charity events, separate prize language from fundraising language so attendees understand where money is going.

## Volunteers

Poker runs need reliable volunteers.

Common roles:

- Event lead
- Registration lead
- Route lead
- Sweep
- Checkpoint volunteers
- Scorekeeper
- Prize lead
- Photographer or content owner
- End-location host

Give every volunteer a written role, arrival time, address, and contact number. Volunteers should not have to infer the plan while participants are arriving.

## Timing

Build a run-of-show.

Include:

- Volunteer arrival
- Registration opens
- Rider briefing
- Start window
- Checkpoint open and close times
- Final arrival deadline
- Scoring window
- Prize announcement
- Event close

Use realistic travel time and add buffer. A poker run that feels rushed becomes less safe and less fun.

## Safety

Safety planning should account for the full route and the checkpoint behavior.

Confirm:

- Route difficulty
- Weather review
- Gas availability
- Rest and water access
- Emergency contacts
- Clear instructions for riders who separate
- No alcohol consumption before or during riding
- Plan for mechanical issues or rider dropouts

If the event includes social time with alcohol at the end, the ride portion should be fully complete first.

## Wrap-Up

After the run:

- Thank riders, volunteers, venues, and sponsors
- Announce winners clearly
- Confirm any donation totals
- Share recap photos
- Archive score rules, route notes, and volunteer notes
- Record improvements in [Lessons-Learned.md](Lessons-Learned.md)

Use [Event-Recap.md](Event-Recap.md) while details are still fresh.
