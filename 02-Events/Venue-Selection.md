# Venue Selection

Venue selection affects safety, attendance, first impressions, and whether the chapter is invited back. A strong venue does not have to be fancy. It needs to fit the event and treat the group well.

Use this with [Meet-and-Greet-Planning.md](Meet-and-Greet-Planning.md), [Ride-Planning.md](Ride-Planning.md), and [Special-Events.md](Special-Events.md).

## Parking

Motorcycle parking is the first filter.

Evaluate:

- Number of bike spaces
- Car parking for non-riders or passengers
- Surface quality
- Lighting
- Slope
- Entry and exit sightlines
- Overflow options
- Whether the group can park together without blocking access

If parking is limited, decide whether the event should require RSVP, use staggered arrival, or move to a better location.

## Motorcycle Friendliness

A motorcycle-friendly venue understands that bikes are part of the event.

Look for:

- Staff openness to motorcycle groups
- Clear rules about where to park
- No history of conflict with riders
- Tolerance for helmets and gear
- Safe space for photos without disrupting business
- Reasonable noise expectations

Motorcycle friendliness does not mean the group can ignore venue rules.

## Food And Drink

Food and drink keep people comfortable and support the host venue.

Confirm:

- Food availability
- Non-alcoholic options
- Water access
- Price range
- Ordering flow for groups
- Whether reservations or tabs are possible
- Whether outside food is prohibited

If alcohol is present, communication should make it clear that riding decisions remain individual and safety-first.

## Accessibility

Accessible events reach more members.

Consider:

- Distance from parking to gathering area
- Seating
- Restrooms
- Lighting
- Noise level
- Heat or cold exposure
- Car access
- Public arrival clarity

For social events, motorcycles should be welcome but not required unless the event truly depends on riding.

## Beginner Friendliness

A beginner-friendly venue is easy to enter, park, and leave.

Avoid venues where newer riders must handle:

- Steep or uneven parking
- Tight gravel lots
- Complex multi-lane exits
- Heavy traffic with poor sightlines
- High-pressure group parking maneuvers

For beginner rides, the destination should reduce stress at the end of the route, not add it.

## Group Size

Match the venue to realistic attendance.

Ask:

- Can the venue handle the expected group?
- Is there room if attendance doubles?
- Will the group overwhelm staff?
- Is seating necessary?
- Is a reservation required?
- Can attendees move around without blocking service?

Recurring events should track attendance trends so venue choices stay realistic.

## Photography Potential

Photography is useful, but it should not outweigh safety or hospitality.

Good photo venues have:

- Clean exterior or recognizable setting
- Safe bike staging
- Good light
- Minimal conflict with customers or traffic
- Permission or tolerance for casual photos

If a venue is great for photos but poor for parking, it may be better as a short photo stop than a full event location.

## Repeat Value

Repeat venues help members build habits and give the chapter reliable anchors.

Track:

- Staff experience
- Parking reliability
- Attendance response
- Food and drink quality
- Cost
- Location convenience
- Seasonal issues
- Whether the venue thanked or welcomed the group back

A good repeat venue should still be reviewed periodically. What worked for 15 people may not work for 50.
